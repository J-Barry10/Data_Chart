NYC's Poorest Students Most Affected by Absenteeism

By: Jarrod Barry
Nov. 17

More than 4 years after the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted schools across the country, students are still struggling with the aftereffects. Amidst reports of some of the lowest test scores in decades and concerns about the ever-present distraction of smartphones, schools nationwide are also battling stubbornly high rates of chronic absenteeism.

Ever since the coronavirus-induced lockdowns that took place during the 2019-20 and 2020-21 school years, schools across the country have struggled to keep students in the classroom. The issue was highlighted in a report last month from the New York state comptroller. The report found that in the 2022-23 school year, nearly a third of students in New York state were “chronically absent”, a term defined as missing 10 percent or more of the school year (typically 18 days in a 180-day year). In New York City specifically, the rate is slightly higher, at around 35%, according to the September Mayor’s Management Report.

Even within New York City, though, absenteeism can vary widely among the city’s 32 districts. The chart below shows how wide the discrepancy can be:

As the chart shows, absenteeism is not evenly spread across the city. For instance, the district with the highest rate – mostly encompassing schools in central Harlem and Morningside Heights – had more than double the chronic absenteeism rate of the district comprising Little Neck and Bayside, the district with the lowest rate. This is emblematic of the gulf that exists within New York City’s schools.

For Dr. Anthony Picciano, a professor in education at Hunter College, the results are not surprising. “While the numbers are higher in New York City, in general, absenteeism is always higher in large urban areas with substantially poor, minority, and immigrant populations,” he said. “The reasons for this are complex but relate to larger societal and family issues.”

Many of the districts with the highest absenteeism rates also have higher levels of poverty. For example, the district with the 2nd-highest absenteeism rates includes the neighborhoods of Bedford Stuyvesant and Weeksville, areas which have poverty rates north of 21%, higher than the city average. Two of the other top-five districts are in the Bronx in areas that have poverty rates over 30%, some of the highest in the city.

In their analysis of Department of Education data, Chalkbeat found that schools with higher percentages of impoverished students faced higher chronic absenteeism rates both before and since the pandemic. According to Picciano, this tracks with what he’s seen over the years.

“The rushed deployment of remote learning was problematic to say the least," he said. "It was most problematic for poor and minority students who had little or no wifi access and felt disconnected from their educations.”
